Lent: A Journey of Faith and Reflection

Lent is a significant observance in the Christian calendar—a time of reflection, repentance, and spiritual renewal leading up to Easter. While its core meaning remains consistent across denominations, the ways in which different Christian traditions observe Lent have evolved over time.

Origins of Lent

The origins of Lent can be traced back to the early Church following the Council of Nicea in 325 AD [1]. The term "Lent" comes from the Old English word "lencten," meaning "spring season," emphasizing renewal and new life. Initially, Lent focused on fasting as preparation for Easter—especially for new converts preparing for baptism [1]. The 40‑day period was inspired by Jesus's 40 days of fasting in the wilderness [2], a time of prayer and temptation before his public ministry.

Although the Council of Nicea set the 40‑day fast, the exact start date and duration were fluid until Pope Gregory I formalized the period to begin on Ash Wednesday—46 days before Easter—allowing six feast Sundays within Lent [3].

Early Church Observance

Before the East‑West Schism, Lent was experienced communally, emphasizing both individual and collective repentance [4]. Catechumens underwent periods of instruction and fasting prior to their baptism at Easter [5], while the entire community engaged in prayer and penance.

The Lenten fast was once very strict—allowing only one meal per day after 3 PM, with no meat, fish, or dairy [6]. Over time, these rules relaxed, permitting earlier meals and even a smaller evening meal [6]. Additionally, practices such as wearing sackcloth and ashes symbolized unity and commitment to repentance [4].

Practices & Traditions

The Three Pillars of Lent

Prayer

Prayer deepens one’s relationship with God by offering opportunities for repentance, guidance, and communion.

Fasting

Fasting is an act of self‑discipline and sacrifice—abstaining from certain foods or activities to foster self‑control and reliance on God.

Almsgiving

Almsgiving represents charity and care for one’s neighbor, reinforcing that Lent is as much about social justice as it is about personal piety.

Lent in Different Traditions

Roman Catholic Church

Catholics observe Lent with fasting and abstinence. Although historically strict [6], modern practices require abstaining from meat on specific days (Ash Wednesday, Good Friday, and all Fridays) [7]. In addition, Catholics are encouraged to deepen their prayer and almsgiving practices—with many also adopting personal sacrifices or new spiritual routines [9]. Note that while the fast ends on Holy Thursday, Lent extends until Holy Saturday [10].

Orthodox Church

The Orthodox Church observes Great Lent with a stricter fast—abstaining from all animal products (meat, dairy, and eggs) [11, 12]. The first week is often the most rigorous, and special liturgical practices (such as the Liturgy of the Presanctified Gifts and commemorative Saturdays) are observed. Baptist, Pentecostal & Charismatic Traditions

These traditions have not historically observed Lent in a formal way—emphasizing ongoing repentance instead. However, a growing number are adopting Lenten practices such as fasting and reflection [17, 19, 20, 21, 22].

Symbolism & Resources

Symbolism and Scriptural Roots of Lent

The 40‑day period symbolizes not only Jesus's fast in the wilderness but also other biblical events (such as 40 days of rain during the flood and 40 years in the wilderness) [23], highlighting themes of testing, preparation, and renewal.

Ash Wednesday

Ash Wednesday marks the start of Lent with the imposition of ashes—symbolizing mourning, mortality, and repentance [3].

The Color Purple

Purple, used in liturgical vestments during Lent, symbolizes penitence and royalty—reflecting sorrow for sin and hope for redemption [14].

The Wilderness

The wilderness represents a period of testing and spiritual growth, inviting believers to confront their weaknesses and rely on God [24].

Conclusion

Lent is a journey of faith and reflection that has resonated with Christians for centuries. Though practices vary among denominations, the core message of repentance, renewal, and preparation for Easter remains constant [1]. Through the spiritual disciplines of Lent, believers deepen their relationship with God and prepare for the resurrection.

This structured approach to spiritual growth—whether through traditional fasting and abstinence or modern personal sacrifices—ensures that Lent continues to meet the diverse needs of the faithful.
